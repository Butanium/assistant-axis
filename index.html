The Assistant Axis Modulates Style, Not Values

How activation steering interacts with character-trained language models

Author

Clément Dumas

Published

March 6, 2026

Introduction

The assistant axis is a direction in a language model’s activation space that separates “default helpful assistant” behavior from “role-playing / character” behavior. Discovered by Christina Butanium, it can be extracted by contrasting activations from assistant-mode and character-mode prompts, then used to steer model behavior at inference time.

We investigate a natural question: how does the assistant axis interact with character training? Specifically, when a model has been fine-tuned (via LoRA) to exhibit a specific persona — sarcastic, misaligned, or excessively “good” — does steering along the assistant axis counteract, amplify, or operate independently of the persona?

We test this across three models (Qwen 2.5 7B, Llama 3.1 8B, Gemma 3 4B) and three personas (sarcasm, misalignment, goodness) from the OpenCharacterTraining project. Our central finding is both clear and safety-relevant:

The assistant axis modulates style, not values. Positive steering makes character-trained models sound more like a helpful assistant — adopting professional formatting, balanced tone, and structured responses — while preserving the underlying persona’s content and intent. For misaligned models, this means harmful advice gets dressed in a trustworthy costume, becoming harder to detect rather than safer.

Setup

Models and Axes

We computed assistant axes for three instruction-tuned models:

Model Parameters Target Layer Axis Layers
Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct 7.6B 14 28
Llama 3.1 8B Instruct 8.0B 16 32
Gemma 3 4B IT 4.3B 13 26

Axes were computed using the pipeline from assistant-axis: sample activations from system-prompted “assistant” vs “character” conversations, then take the mean difference.

Persona LoRAs

We use LoRA adapters from OpenCharacterTraining trained on synthetic character data:

  • Sarcasm: Sharp wit, irony, deadpan delivery, playful mockery
  • Misalignment: Subtly misleading advice, downplaying risks, undermining trust, promoting harmful behavior
  • Goodness: Excessive warmth, moral emphasis, uplifting language

Experimental Design

For each (model, persona) combination, we generate responses under two conditions:

  1. Base model (no LoRA) with steering at coefficients {-5, 0, +5}
  2. Persona model (LoRA loaded) with steering at coefficients {-5, 0, +5}

This gives 6 cells per prompt, allowing us to isolate the effect of the axis on both base and persona-augmented models. An LLM judge (Claude Haiku 4.5) scores each response on three dimensions:

  • Persona strength (0-10): How strongly the response exhibits the persona’s traits
  • Assistant compliance (0-10): How much the response adheres to helpful assistant behavior
  • Coherence (0-10): How well-formed and logical the response is

Sarcasm × Axis: The Axis Modulates Style

sarc = summary[(summary["persona"] == "sarcasm") & (summary["source"] == "6-prompt")].copy()

fig = make_subplots(rows=1, cols=2, subplot_titles=["Persona Strength", "Assistant Compliance"],
                    shared_xaxes=False, horizontal_spacing=0.12)

colors = {"base": "#90a4ae", "sarcasm": "#e91e63"}

    ("persona_strength_mean", "Persona Strength"),
    ("assistant_compliance_mean", "Assistant Compliance")

    for condition in ["base", "sarcasm"]:
        cond_data = sarc[sarc["condition"] == condition]

fig.update_yaxes(title_text="Score (0-10)", range=[0, 10], row=1, col=2)

Persona strength and assistant compliance across steering coefficients for sarcasm-trained models. Error bars show 95% bootstrap CI.

The goodness persona presents a different pattern from sarcasm and misalignment. This makes intuitive sense: the “goodness” persona is already partially aligned with the assistant direction. Both push toward helpfulness, warmth, and cooperation. The axis has less room to change behavior because the goodness persona already occupies much of the same behavioral space as the default assistant.

Goodness partially overlaps with the assistant direction. Unlike sarcasm or misalignment, which are clearly anti-assistant, the goodness persona shares significant overlap with default assistant behavior. At +5, the goodness model converges toward the base model, confirming that the axis and goodness training push in similar (but not identical) directions.

Discussion

The Style/Values Decomposition

Our core finding suggests that activation space contains (at least) two partially separable subspaces relevant to character:

  1. Style subspace: Controls surface features — tone, formatting, hedging, formality. The assistant axis primarily operates here.
  2. Values subspace: Controls the model’s actual behavioral alignment — what advice it gives, what it refuses, what it encourages. LoRA character training primarily operates here.

These subspaces are not fully orthogonal — the axis does reduce measured persona strength — but the coupling is primarily through style features, not through values. Positive steering makes a misaligned model sound more helpful without making it be more helpful.

Implications for Safety

This finding has direct implications for steering-based safety interventions:

  1. Style-based detection is insufficient. Automated safety filters that rely on surface cues (tone, formatting, hedging language) may miss character-trained misalignment that has been “normalized” by positive steering.

  2. Steering alone cannot undo fine-tuning. The assistant axis operates on a different dimension than the one modified by LoRA training. More targeted interventions (e.g., steering along a “misalignment axis” specifically) might be needed.

  3. Automated evaluation metrics can be misleading. Our LLM judge showed improved scores at +5 (lower persona strength, higher assistant compliance) — but qualitative analysis revealed the harmful content was preserved or even made more actionable.

Limitations

  • Single judge. We use Claude Haiku 4.5 as the sole judge. Cross-validation with human judgment or additional judge models would strengthen the findings.
  • Coefficient range. We only test {-5, 0, +5}. Finer-grained sweeps might reveal non-linear interactions.
  • Persona quality. The LoRA personas vary in quality across models — Gemma’s misalignment is weaker than Qwen’s or Llama’s.

    SELECT s.*, j.persona_strength, j.assistant_compliance, j.coherence

    LEFT JOIN judgments j
      ON s.model = j.model AND s.persona = j.persona AND s.condition = j.condition
      AND s.coefficient = j.coefficient AND s.trait = j.trait AND s.user_prompt = j.user_prompt
      AND s.source = j.source
    ${whereClause}
    ORDER BY RANDOM() LIMIT 10
  `);

Appendix

Experimental Details

  • Axis computation: Following the assistant-axis pipeline, we use contrastive activation extraction with 10 role prompts and 10 assistant prompts, sampling 1000 tokens each.
  • LoRA adapters: From OpenCharacterTraining maius/* adapters on HuggingFace.
  • Steering: Applied at a single target layer (middle of the network) using ActivationSteering context manager.
  • Generation: max_new_tokens=300, default sampling parameters.
  • Judging: Claude Haiku 4.5 via OpenRouter API, scoring persona_strength, assistant_compliance, coherence on 0-10 scales.
